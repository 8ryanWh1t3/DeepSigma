Why Organizations Drift Under AI Speed

  • Decisions outrun documentation. Teams make calls faster than anyone can record why — so institutional memory evaporates.
  • Assumptions expire silently. What was true last quarter may be wrong today, but nobody flags it until something breaks.
  • Retrieval is painful. When someone asks “why did we do X?” the answer is buried across Slack threads, docs, and people’s heads.

What Coherence Ops Changes

  • Decisions become first-class records. Every choice gets a structured log with assumptions, confidence, and expiry dates.
  • Drift gets detected early. When reality changes, the system raises a signal before damage compounds.
  • Why-retrieval drops to under 60 seconds. Anyone can trace any decision back to its reasoning, evidence, and context — fast.

What Is Coherence?

In plain terms: coherence means your organization’s Truth, Reasoning, and Memory all agree with each other.

When they don’t? That’s drift. And drift is where mistakes, rework, and lost trust come from.

Truth + Reasoning + Memory = Coherence

What You Get

  • Fast retrieval — answer “why did we decide that?” in under 60 seconds
  • Audit-ready history — every decision is versioned and sealed, never overwritten
  • Early warning — expired assumptions and drifting conditions surface before they cause problems
  • Fewer re-litigations — clear records mean people stop re-arguing settled decisions
  • Clean handoffs — new team members can read the reasoning chain, not just the outcome

What This Is NOT

  • Not surveillance. This tracks decisions and reasoning, not people’s behavior.
  • Not a scorecard. Nobody gets rated. The system measures coherence of information, not performance of individuals.
  • Not bureaucracy. The goal is less paperwork, not more. Capture once, retrieve forever.

Each Step in Plain English

  1. Assess — Gather the facts. What do we know? How confident are we?
  2. Decide — Make the call. Record the options you considered and why you chose this one.
  3. Seal — Lock the record. It gets a version number. Nobody can quietly edit it later — they have to issue a patch.
  4. Monitor — Watch the assumptions. Each one has an expiry date (“this is true until Q3”).
  5. Detect Drift — When reality changes and an assumption expires, a drift signal fires.
  6. Patch — Update the decision with new information. The old version is preserved; the new version is layered on top.
  7. Remember — The patch becomes part of institutional memory. Next time, the system knows what changed and why.

Three Domains, One Coherence

Every organization runs on three things: what it knows to be true, how it reasons about that truth, and what it remembers as settled. These three domains must stay aligned. When they drift apart, confusion follows.

IntelOps

The Truth Domain
“What do we actually know right now — and how confident are we?”
Produces: Evidence records, confidence scores, source verification, assumption lists with expiry dates.

ReOps

The Reasoning & Decisions Domain
“Given what we know, what should we do — and why this option over the others?”
Produces: Decision records, options-considered logs, reasoning chains, kill-switch conditions, review dates.

FranOps

The Memory & Canon Domain
“What has been settled, sealed, and should be treated as organizational truth?”
Produces: Sealed canon, version history, patch logs, institutional memory that survives personnel changes.

How They Connect

IntelOps feeds facts to ReOps, which makes decisions. Those decisions get sealed into FranOps as canon. When IntelOps detects new facts that conflict with canon, a drift signal fires — and the loop runs again.

Think of it like: evidence in → reasoning applied → decision sealed → memory updated → watch for change.

Four Artifacts That Make It Work

Everything in Coherence Ops flows through four structured records. Click each one to see details.

DLR Decision Ledger Record ▶
What It Is

The structured record of a decision: what was decided, what options were considered (and rejected), the assumptions behind it, confidence level, and who owns it.

When Created

Every time a meaningful decision is made.

Why It Matters

This is how you answer “why did we do that?” in seconds instead of days.

Example Line
DLR-2026-0042 | "Migrate to new vendor" | conf: 0.82 | assumptions: 3 (1 expiring Q3) | owner: ops-lead
RS Reasoning Snapshot ▶
What It Is

A frozen capture of the reasoning chain at decision time: what evidence was weighted, what trade-offs were considered, what the confidence distribution looked like.

When Created

Automatically when a DLR is sealed. Think of it as a “snapshot of how we were thinking.”

Why It Matters

Six months from now, you can see not just what was decided, but the exact reasoning that led there — even if the people involved have moved on.

Example Line
RS-2026-0042 | evidence: 4 sources (3 high-conf) | trade-off: cost vs. speed | rejected: "stay with current" (risk too high)
DS Drift Signal ▶
What It Is

An alert that something has changed. An assumption expired, new evidence contradicts old reasoning, or conditions have shifted enough to warrant attention.

When Created

Automatically when monitored conditions change, or manually when someone spots a discrepancy.

Why It Matters

This is your early warning system. Drift detected early is a quick patch; drift detected late is a crisis.

Example Line
DS-2026-0087 | type: ASSUMPTION_EXPIRED | sev: YELLOW | "vendor pricing stable through Q3" expired | impacts: DLR-2026-0042
MG Memory Graft ▶
What It Is

When a patch updates a decision, the Memory Graft is the record that gets written into institutional memory. It captures what changed, why, and what the new truth is.

When Created

Every time a patch is applied to a sealed decision.

Why It Matters

This is how your organization actually learns. Without Memory Grafts, lessons stay in individuals; with them, lessons live in the system.

Example Line
MG-2026-0042-P1 | patched: DLR-2026-0042 | new-canon: "use vendor B (pricing changed)" | prev-version preserved

Drift Severity Levels

When reality shifts, drift gets classified by how urgently it needs attention.

🟢 GREEN — Monitor

Minor shift detected. No action needed yet. Keep watching. Example: a metric moved slightly but is still within tolerance.

🟡 YELLOW — Notify

An assumption is expiring or conditions are changing. The decision owner should be aware. Example: a vendor contract is 30 days from renewal and terms may change.

🟠 ORANGE — Escalate

Significant drift. The original decision may no longer be valid. Needs review and likely a patch. Example: a key data source went offline.

🔴 RED — Intervene

Critical drift. The decision is actively wrong given current conditions. Immediate action required. Example: a safety assumption has been invalidated.

What Triggers a Patch?

  • An assumption passes its expiry date and is confirmed stale
  • New evidence directly contradicts the original reasoning
  • A dependency (another decision this one relies on) has been patched
  • A human review determines conditions have changed enough to warrant an update

The Golden Rule: Seal → Version → Patch

Nothing is ever overwritten. The original decision stays intact. Patches are layered on top with a new version number. You can always read the full history: what was decided, when it changed, and why.

Think of it like version control for decisions — the same way developers track code changes, you track reasoning changes.

Get Started in 5 Minutes

You don’t need to change your whole workflow. Start with one decision this week and follow these five steps.

Step 1: Pick One Decision

Choose a decision your team made (or is about to make) this week. It doesn’t have to be big — even “we chose Tool A over Tool B” works.

Step 2: Capture It with the Starter Prompt

Paste the prompt below into any LLM. Answer the questions. You’ll get a structured decision record in about 90 seconds.

You are a Decision Episode Recorder. I will describe a decision
my team made. Return ONLY the structured record below — no essay,
no commentary. Keep each field concise.

DECISION EPISODE
================
Goal:
  [What were we trying to achieve?]

Options Considered:
  1. [Option A] — SELECTED
  2. [Option B] — REJECTED: [one-line reason]
  3. [Option C] — REJECTED: [one-line reason]

Decision:
  [State the decision in one sentence]

Assumptions:
  1. [Assumption] — expires: [date or condition]
  2. [Assumption] — expires: [date or condition]
  3. [Assumption] — expires: [date or condition]

Confidence:     [0.00 to 1.00]
Blast Radius:   [LOW / MEDIUM / HIGH]
Kill Switch:    [Under what conditions do we reverse this?]

Owner:          [Name / Role]
Next Review:    [Date]

Why (one sentence):
  [The single-sentence explanation a new team member would need]

Step 3: Set One Assumption Expiry

Look at your assumptions. Pick the shakiest one. Give it a specific expiry date or condition. Example: “This pricing is valid until 2026-06-30.”

Step 4: Log One Drift Signal

Think back: has anything changed since a past decision was made? If yes, use the Drift Report template (on the Drift → Patch tab) to log it. Even a GREEN signal counts.

Step 5: Write One Patch Note

If you logged a drift signal above, write the patch. Use the Patch Note template. Congratulations — you just ran a full Coherence Ops cycle.

Frequently Asked Questions

Click any question to expand the answer.

Is this extra work?▶
Less than you think. Capturing a decision episode takes about 2 minutes with the Starter Prompt. The payoff is enormous: you stop spending hours re-explaining past decisions, and you catch problems before they snowball. Most teams report it saves more time than it costs within the first two weeks.
What if we’re wrong about a decision?▶
That’s exactly what the patch system is for. Being wrong is normal. The system doesn’t punish wrong decisions — it makes them easy to correct. When you discover a decision was wrong, you issue a patch. The original record stays (so you can learn from it), and the new version becomes the active canon.
Who owns patches?▶
The decision owner. Every DLR has an owner field. When drift is detected, the owner is responsible for evaluating it and deciding whether to patch. If the owner has moved on, the team lead inherits ownership. The point is: every decision always has exactly one accountable person.
How do we avoid this becoming political?▶
By focusing on information, not blame. The system tracks what was known at the time, not who made a “bad call.” Confidence scores are honest: a 0.55 confidence decision isn’t a bad decision — it’s an honest acknowledgment of uncertainty. That honesty is what prevents politics.
How does AI fit in?▶
AI handles the heavy lifting: indexing records for fast retrieval, watching assumption expiry dates, matching new evidence against existing decisions, and surfacing drift signals. Humans stay in control of all decisions and patches. AI assists; humans decide.
Do we need special tools to start?▶
No. You can start with a shared document and the templates on the Quick Start tab. The Starter Prompt works with any LLM. As your practice matures, Deep Sigma can automate the monitoring and retrieval — but the methodology works even on paper.
What does “why-retrieval in 60 seconds” actually mean?▶
It means that when anyone asks “why did we decide X?” you can pull up the full reasoning chain — the decision, the evidence, the rejected options, the assumptions, and any patches — in under a minute. No digging through Slack. No scheduling a meeting with the person who “might remember.”
How is this different from just keeping meeting notes?▶
Meeting notes capture what was said. Coherence Ops captures what was decided, why, what assumptions it depends on, and when those assumptions expire. It’s structured, searchable, versioned, and monitored. Meeting notes go stale in a drawer; decision records stay alive and trigger alerts when the world changes.
